#### Narrative block 1 body

**EN:** German industrial manufacturing. Norwegian enterprise software. Spanish telecom. I built trust with demanding clients in complex industries — not by undercutting on price, but by being the most consistent creative partner they had. Every market came with its own expectations: precision, clarity, speed. I calibrated production for each without starting from scratch. Geography was never the limit. Reliability was.

**ES:** Industria manufacturera alemana. Software empresarial noruego. Telecomunicaciones españolas. Gané la confianza de clientes exigentes en sectores complejos — no tirando precios, sino siendo el socio creativo más consistente que tenían. Cada mercado traía sus propias exigencias: precisión, claridad, velocidad. Calibré la producción para cada uno sin empezar de cero. La geografía nunca fue el límite. La fiabilidad sí.

#### Narrative block 2 body

**EN:** Corporate training for Siemens. Brand films. Product launches. All delivered simultaneously in up to 7 languages. That means motion systems flexible enough to survive radical text-length shifts, voice talent coordinated across markets, and frame-by-frame QA in every tongue. Most teams outsource the localisation and hope it holds. I designed it so it always did — on time, every language, first pass.

**ES:** Formación corporativa para Siemens. Brand films. Lanzamientos de producto. Todo entregado simultáneamente en hasta 7 idiomas. Eso implica sistemas de motion capaces de sobrevivir a diferencias radicales de longitud de texto, locutores coordinados entre mercados y QA frame a frame en cada lengua. La mayoría de equipos externalizan la localización y rezan. Yo lo diseñé para que siempre funcionara — a tiempo, todos los idiomas, a la primera.

#### Narrative block 3 body

**EN:** The hardest thing about running a studio isn't the best project you ship. It's the 50th being as good as the 5th. I built a production system — sign-off gates, quality checks, review cadences — that held the bar steady across years and teams. No heroics. Just process. When you're delivering at volume, consistency is the hardest thing to earn and the easiest to lose. I kept it.

**ES:** Lo más difícil de dirigir un estudio no es tu mejor proyecto. Es que el número 50 sea tan bueno como el número 5. Construí un sistema de producción — puntos de control, criterios de calidad, ritmos de revisión — que mantuvo el estándar firme durante años y equipos. Sin heroicidades. Solo proceso. Cuando entregas en volumen, la consistencia es lo más difícil de ganar y lo más fácil de perder. Yo la mantuve.

#### Narrative block 4 body

**EN:** Creative brief to final render. 3D artists, motion designers, sound engineers, localisation partners — I was the person connecting every discipline. Not by micromanaging, but because I designed a pipeline where nothing fell through the cracks. Speed doesn't come from overtime. It comes from eliminating the handoff points where projects stall because nobody knows who owns what.

**ES:** Del brief creativo al render final. Artistas 3D, motion designers, ingenieros de sonido, socios de localización — yo era quien conectaba cada disciplina. No microgestionando, sino porque diseñé un pipeline donde nada se caía entre las grietas. La velocidad no sale de hacer horas extra. Sale de eliminar los puntos de handoff donde los proyectos se estancan porque nadie sabe quién es responsable de qué.

**EN**
- Title: Why motion design systems matter more than animated logos
- Category: Motion
- Read time: 5 min
- Description: Most brands treat motion as decoration. A motion design system is infrastructure — it makes every interaction consistent, faster to produce, and actually useful.
- Body: Most brands ask for "something animated" when what they need is a system. An animated logo on the homepage is a one-off. A motion design system is infrastructure that pays for itself across every touchpoint.
  **The problem with one-off animations** — I've seen it dozens of times. A brand spends weeks perfecting a logo reveal. It looks great in the case study. Then the team needs to animate a button, a page transition, a loading state — and they have nothing to work with. Every animator does their own thing. The result feels disjointed.
  **What a motion system actually is** — It's a set of rules. Easing curves, durations, triggers, and behaviors defined once and applied everywhere. Think of it like a type scale but for movement. When a card enters the viewport, you already know how fast it moves, what easing it uses, and how it staggers relative to its neighbors.
  In practice, I define three layers:
  - **Micro-interactions** — button hovers, toggle switches, focus rings. 100-200ms, ease-out.
  - **Transitions** — page loads, section reveals, modal opens. 300-500ms, custom cubic-bezier.
  - **Sequences** — onboarding flows, multi-step reveals, scroll-driven narratives. Timelines with stagger.
  **The payoff** — Once the system exists, production speed doubles. Designers stop debating easing curves. Developers stop hardcoding values. And the brand feels cohesive across web, app, and social — not because someone matched the style, but because the rules are the rules.
  Motion is punctuation, not decoration. Build the grammar first. The sentences write themselves.

  **The bridge role** — This is why I code. Not to replace developers, but to speak their language. When I hand off a design, I can flag the tricky parts. When a developer pushes back, I understand why. The gap between design and implementation shrinks — and the product gets better.
